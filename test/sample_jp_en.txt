left the pharmacy bag on the bus; at first she apparently
thought it was hopeless, but the driver delivered it to the
office, and when she called 011-222-0000, she safely
received it by evening; my mother kept saying, the world
is not something to give up on. The office staff kept the
medicine bag unopened, and at pickup they also checked
her identity; after returning home, my mother declared,
a little embarrassed, that from now on she would always
keep her belongings on her lap.
Dr. Nakamura of Kyoto University Hospital explained that
the new drug for Alzheimer's disease is not a drug that
completely reverses symptoms, but a drug that
examines the possibility of slowing progression; precisely
for that reason, he emphasized that imaging tests before
administration, explanation to the family, and regular
observation to find side effects are indispensable. The
doctor also said that, if the family expects too much
simply from the word new drug, the patient's own daily
rhythm and diet management may be neglected;
treatment is not one injection, but the accumulation of
daily observation.
The Nikkei reported that, while the weak yen continues
and spending by visitors to Japan is growing, local inns
are beginning to limit reservations because of labor
shortages; in other words, strong tourism demand and
the ability of regions to receive that demand are not the
same thing, and a system including transportation,
cleaning, and food procurement is being tested. At the
end, the article pointed out that, even if local
governments only distribute subsidies, the workforce will
not suddenly increase; only after dormitories, childcare,
and training for foreign staff are prepared can the lodging
industry avoid being swallowed by the wave of demand.
